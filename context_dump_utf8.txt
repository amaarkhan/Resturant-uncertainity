1. Product Research (Critical)
Key Question
What exactly must this product do to solve the problem meaningfully?
Key Consideration
In Phase 1, the Lean Model Canvas highlighted the problem of demand uncertainty in small Pakistani restaurants. This section focuses on validating the product viability by understanding user needs, current solutions, and defining what the product should actually do.
A. User Needs & Pain Points
Target Users
The main users of this product are small restaurant owners and kitchen managers in cities such as Karachi, Lahore, Islamabad, and Rawalpindi. These people are responsible for deciding how much food should be prepared each day.
What Users Are Trying to Achieve
Restaurant owners mainly want to:
Prepare the right amount of food daily
Reduce food waste
Avoid running out of popular items
Maintain stable daily profits
Since margins are small, even small mistakes in preparation can affect their income.
Current Struggles
Many small restaurants do not have proper sales data or forecasting tools. Most decisions are based on experience or guesswork.
Demand also changes because of weather, cricket matches, exams, or local events, which are difficult to predict without data.
Because of this, restaurants often face two problems:
Too much food prepared ΓåÆ waste
Too little food prepared ΓåÆ lost sales
These issues also create stress for owners who must make quick daily decisions.
Evidence for these challenges comes from industry reports on food waste, business articles, and common observations in small restaurant operations where manual estimation is the norm.
B. Existing Solutions & Substitutes
Currently, small restaurants manage demand in a few simple ways.
Gut Feeling
Most owners rely on their past experience and intuition.
This works because it is quick and familiar, but it often fails when unexpected events affect customer demand.
Manual Records
Some restaurants maintain notebooks or Excel sheets to track sales.
These can help record past data, but they still do not consider outside factors like weather or events.
POS Systems
Some larger restaurants use POS or inventory software.
These tools track sales well, but they are often expensive and complex for small restaurants, and they focus more on reporting than predicting future demand.
No Digital Tools
Many small restaurants simply do not use any software, relying completely on routine and experience.
This shows a clear gap for a simple decision-support tool designed specifically for small businesses.
C. Product Scope & Differentiation
What the Product Must Do
The product should act as a simple assistant that helps restaurant owners decide how much food to prepare each day.
It will:
Use signals such as weather, local events, holidays, and exam schedules
Provide daily preparation suggestions for key menu items
Offer a simple mobile dashboard that owners can quickly check before starting the day
Allow quick feedback like ΓÇ£ran out earlyΓÇ¥ or ΓÇ£too much leftΓÇ¥ so the system can improve over time
The goal is to provide clear, practical guidance, not complex analytics.
What the Product Will Not Do
To keep the product focused, it will not try to replace full restaurant management software.
It will not include:
POS systems
Inventory management tools
Accounting features
Staff scheduling
These tools already exist and solving them would make the product unnecessarily complex.
How This Product Is Different
The main difference is that this solution focuses on context-aware preparation planning.
Instead of only tracking past sales, it looks at external signals like weather, events, and exam schedules to suggest preparation quantities.
It is also designed specifically for small restaurants with limited data and technical resources, making it simple and easy to use.
2. Business Research
Key Question
Why could this exist as a sustainable product or business?
Key Consideration
In Phase 1, the Business Model Canvas and Value Chain helped outline how the product might operate as a business. This section focuses on validating business viability by examining customer behavior, revenue logic, and potential risks.
A. Customer & Buyer Dynamics
Who Uses the Product vs Who Pays
The main users of the product are restaurant owners and kitchen managers who make daily decisions about food preparation.
In most small restaurants, the owner is also the decision maker and the one who pays for software tools. This means the product must clearly show financial value to the owner.
How Purchasing Decisions Are Made
Small restaurant owners usually make independent and quick purchasing decisions. Their choices are mostly based on:
Cost of the product
Ease of use
Immediate practical benefits
If the product clearly helps reduce food waste or increase sales, owners are more likely to consider using it.
Incentives and Constraints
The main incentive for adopting the product is saving money by reducing waste and improving preparation planning.
However, there are also some constraints:
Some owners have limited experience with digital tools
There may be resistance to paying monthly subscriptions
Owners may hesitate to trust predictions from a new system
Because of this, the product must remain simple, affordable, and easy to try.
B. Revenue & Cost Logic (High-Level)
Pricing of Similar Products
Many restaurant software tools follow a monthly subscription model, especially SaaS platforms for POS systems, inventory management, or analytics.
These tools are often priced based on the number of locations or features offered. For small businesses, pricing is usually kept low and predictable to encourage adoption.
A similar approach could work for this product, offering simple subscription plans for small restaurant owners.
Willingness to Pay
Restaurant owners are more willing to pay for tools that directly improve profits or reduce losses.
Since food waste in restaurants can reach 10ΓÇô15% of prepared food, even a small improvement in preparation accuracy could translate into noticeable cost savings. This creates a reasonable case for owners to consider paying for a helpful planning tool.
Major Cost Drivers
The main costs involved in running the product would include:
Product development and ongoing improvements
Cloud hosting and infrastructure
Data services, such as weather or event information
System maintenance and updates
Customer support and onboarding
These costs are typical for digital SaaS products and can scale as more users join the platform.
C. Business Risks & Constraints
Operational Risks
One risk is that prediction accuracy may not always be reliable, especially in the early stages when limited data is available. If recommendations are wrong, restaurant owners may stop using the product.
Trust and Adoption Risks
Restaurant owners may be skeptical of automated recommendations. Building trust will require clear explanations and consistent results.
Compliance and Data Concerns
If the product collects restaurant sales data in the future, it will need to ensure basic data privacy and responsible handling of business information.
Scaling Limitations
As the product grows, it may need more local data sources and improved models to maintain accuracy across different cities and restaurant types.
The system may also depend on external data sources, such as weather or event information, which could affect reliability if those sources change.
3. Market / Industry Research (Critical)
Key Question
Why does this problem matter in the broader market, and why now?
Key Consideration
This research mainly relies on secondary research such as industry reports, articles on restaurant operations, and market trends in the food service sector. These sources help understand the scale of the problem and the current solutions available.
A. Market Context
Target Market
The main target market is small and medium-sized restaurants and food stalls in major Pakistani cities such as Karachi, Lahore, Islamabad, and Rawalpindi. These businesses usually operate with small teams and limited technology support.
Market Size
Exact numbers are difficult to determine, but industry estimates suggest that tens of thousands of small food businesses operate in major urban areas of Pakistan. Many of these businesses rely on manual operations and simple decision-making processes.
Even if only a small portion of these restaurants adopt digital tools, the potential market for a simple preparation planning solution could still be significant.
Industry Trends
Several trends make this problem more relevant today:
Increasing food delivery and cloud kitchen businesses
Growing use of mobile apps in small businesses
Rising food and ingredient prices
Increasing interest in reducing food waste
These trends suggest that restaurants are slowly becoming more open to tools that improve efficiency and reduce losses.
B. Competitive Landscape
Direct Competitors
Direct competitors include restaurant analytics platforms and demand forecasting tools used in larger restaurant chains. These tools often provide sales forecasting and inventory tracking.
However, they are usually designed for larger businesses with full POS systems and may be too complex or expensive for small restaurants.
Indirect Competitors
Indirect competitors include:
POS systems with basic reporting
Inventory management software
Restaurant management platforms
These tools help with tracking operations but usually do not focus on predicting daily demand based on external factors.
Non-Consumption / Manual Alternatives
The most common alternative today is manual decision making.
Restaurant owners often rely on:
Experience and intuition
Fixed preparation routines
Simple notes or spreadsheets
These approaches are familiar but often fail when unexpected factors affect customer demand.
Who Wins Today and Why
Currently, larger restaurant chains benefit from POS systems and analytics tools because they have access to structured data and larger budgets.
Small restaurants, however, are mostly underserved by these tools due to cost, complexity, and lack of technical resources.
Where Gaps Exist
There is a gap for simple, affordable tools designed specifically for small restaurants that do not have large data systems but still need better preparation planning.
C. Structural Barriers
Switching Costs
Some restaurants may already use basic POS or inventory tools. Switching to a new system requires time and effort, which may discourage adoption.
Regulatory Constraints
There are generally few strict regulations affecting restaurant management software, but businesses must still handle operational data responsibly.
Cultural Resistance
One of the biggest barriers is behavioral and cultural resistance. Many restaurant owners trust their experience more than digital recommendations, which could slow adoption.
4. Economic Research (Critical)
Key Question
Why does this problem matter in the economic market right now?
Economic Context
Several economic conditions make this problem more important today.
Rising Food Prices
Food prices and ingredient costs have increased significantly in recent years. For small restaurants, even small amounts of waste can directly affect profit margins.
Inflation Pressure
High inflation increases operating costs for small businesses, making efficiency and cost control more important than before.
Uncertain Demand
Economic uncertainty also affects customer spending patterns, which can make daily demand even more unpredictable.
Because of these factors, restaurant owners are under pressure to reduce waste and make better preparation decisions.
Assumptions & Evidence
During the research process, several assumptions from earlier phases were examined.
Assumption 1
Small restaurants experience significant food waste due to inaccurate preparation planning.
Evidence: Industry reports and food service studies commonly mention 10ΓÇô15% food waste levels in restaurants, supporting this assumption.
Assumption 2
External factors such as weather or events affect customer demand.
Evidence: Observations and industry examples show that demand often increases during events and decreases during poor weather conditions.
Assumption 3
Small restaurant owners may benefit from simple decision-support tools.
Evidence: The lack of accessible forecasting tools for small businesses suggests an opportunity for simpler solutions.
High-Risk Assumptions
Some assumptions still remain uncertain:
Whether restaurant owners will use the tool consistently
Whether they will be willing to pay for such a solution
Whether predictions will be accurate enough to build trust
These uncertainties will need to be tested in later phases.
Phase 2 Summary
Confirmed Assumptions
Research suggests that:
Demand uncertainty is a real issue for small restaurants
Food waste has a direct financial impact
Most small restaurants lack forecasting tools
These findings support the core problem identified in earlier phases.
Remaining Risks
Some uncertainties remain, including:
Adoption behavior among small restaurant owners
Willingness to pay for a new tool
Trust in automated recommendations
These risks will need to be tested through practical product trials.
Product Scope Refinement
Based on the research, the product scope remains focused on a simple tool that helps restaurant owners plan daily food preparation more accurately.
Complex features such as full restaurant management systems are intentionally excluded to keep the product simple and accessible.

MVP Focus for Phase 3
The MVP will focus on testing the most important assumptions by providing:
Simple daily preparation recommendations
Use of a limited number of signals such as weather and events
A basic interface for restaurant owners to check suggestions and provide feedback
The MVP will not include complex analytics or full operational software, as the goal is to first validate whether demand guidance alone provides value.
